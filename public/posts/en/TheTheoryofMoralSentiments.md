## ——On Resentment, Justice, Religion, and Inner Conflict: I Finally Found a Coherent Explanation

When I first opened *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, I didn't have particularly high expectations. When many people think of Adam Smith, the first things that come to mind are economics, *The Wealth of Nations*, markets, division of labor, and the invisible hand. Few would associate him with someone suffering from long-term mental strain and inner conflict. But after reading it, my deepest feeling was this:

> **This book didn't teach me how to succeed, but it explained why I was in pain.**

For a long time in the past, I carried an indescribable sense of imbalance toward the world. I couldn't understand why some despicable, arrogant, manipulative people often navigated social situations effortlessly; why sincere people were frequently hurt, yet the so-called "retribution" never seemed to come. I tried to find answers in religion, philosophy, and inspirational narratives, but always felt a piece of the puzzle was missing. It wasn't until *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* appeared that I realized: what was trapping me wasn't the emotions themselves, but my misunderstanding of the world's operating logic.

---

## I. Why Resentment Often Fails to Garner Support: Because Emotions Have Their Own Social Rules

A passage in the book about "resentment and sympathy" held my attention for a long time. The author points out that when a person is harmed, anger is often a natural and reasonable reaction. But bystanders do not automatically side with the angry person. On the contrary, if the person's emotions are too intense, bystanders may even withdraw, feel repulsed, or think they have "lost control." Reading this sentence was initially uncomfortable because it felt so cold — it almost said:

> **Even if a person has been wronged, they cannot freely express their grievance.**

In the past, I was very resistant to this idea. It felt like asking the victim to continue restraining themselves, causing secondary harm.

But as I continued reading, I began to understand that what he was really trying to explain wasn't a moral requirement, but a social mechanism. Sympathy between people does not occur directly. It requires the bystander to be able to "imagine themselves in your position" and align emotionally with you. If your anger has already exceeded what they can bear, they cannot enter your situation; they will only feel the pressure first. In other words, support from others depends not only on whether you are right, but also on whether you can help others understand your reasoning. This isn't romantic, but it is very realistic.

I later realized that many people fail in relationships not because they are wrong, but because they mistake "authentic expression" for "effective expression." Smith helped me understand one thing:

> **The sincerity of emotion is not the same as the effectiveness of emotion.**

Sometimes restraint isn't weakness; it's a way for the truth to be seen.

## II. Why Bad People Often Seem to Do Well: Because the World Operates on Rules First, Not Retribution

One of my biggest sources of inner conflict in the past was my anger over "the wicked going unpunished." Those lacking virtue remained respectable; those who hurt others still lived glamorously; those skilled at performance were still surrounded by applause. Whenever I saw these phenomena, I felt a deep sense of imbalance. Then, a discussion in the book about "justice and benevolence" completely changed my perspective.

The author argues that the core foundation of society is not benevolence, but justice. A society lacking warmth might still function; but if injustice spreads comprehensively, the very structure of society will collapse. This sentence cut through many illusions like a knife. I had previously assumed the world should operate in real-time according to the logic of "good is rewarded with good, evil with evil." But this book reminded me that the primary task of a real society is not to reward virtue, but to maintain basic order.

This means:

*A person who is mean, selfish, or vain may not necessarily pay an immediate price;*
*As long as they haven't crossed the bottom line of the system, the system may still accommodate their continued existence.*

This isn't because the world approves of them, but because the world prioritizes "whether it can function" over "whether it is noble."

When I understood this, a long-held anger inside me began to loosen. Because I finally realized:

> **The social system is not a moral fairy tale. It pursues stability first, and beauty second.**

This doesn't make one cynical; rather, it makes one clearer-headed. You no longer pin your hopes on some mysterious retribution, nor do you doubt the entire world because of short-term imbalances.

## III. Why Religion Has Persisted So Long: Because Humanity Needs a Court Beyond Reality

I have read many religious classics and have often been puzzled: why do religions across different civilizations so often repeat similar narratives of good and evil, judgment, and redemption?

*The Theory of Moral Sentiments* gave me a highly explanatory answer. The author speaks of an "impartial spectator" within the human heart. It acts like an internal judge, allowing us to determine whether our actions are appropriate and worthy of respect. But this spectator is not always stable. When a person suffers long-term misunderstanding, humiliation, isolation — when their entire real environment seems to stand against them — their internal judgment system begins to waver. They begin to doubt themselves, feel that innocence is meaningless, and experience loneliness so profound it feels like collapse. It is at this point that the function of religion emerges. It may not provide new scientific knowledge, but rather a space for judgment that transcends reality — a place where justice ultimately still exists.

That is, one reason religion has persisted is that it fulfills a deep psychological need in humanity:

> **When the real court fails, people still need to believe that judgment has not disappeared.**

This understanding allowed me, for the first time, to view religion from a socio-psychological perspective. It is not merely a belief system, but also a kind of spiritual order. It enables those who have been suppressed in reality to still maintain their sense of dignity.

## IV. The Key to Stopping Inner Conflict: Establishing a Stable Spectator Within

Throughout this entire book, the concept that helped me the most was the "impartial spectator." The reason I was prone to inner conflict in the past was that my evaluation system was overly externalized. I cared too much about how others saw me, was too easily defined by my environment, and too easily fell into self-doubt because of a single person's disapproval. This meant I had handed over the right to judge my life to the fluctuating crowd.

The author proposes that a person can cultivate a more stable observer within their own mind. This observer is not a specific person, but a rational, neutral perspective that neither panders excessively nor criticizes excessively. When I got into the habit of viewing myself from this position, a subtle but profound change occurred.

I started asking not:

"Will others like me?"
"Will they misunderstand me?"
"Have I made everyone satisfied?"

But rather:

*"If I were a fair person, would I approve of my actions at this moment?"*

This is a seemingly simple but immensely powerful shift in perspective. Because it reclaims the power of evaluation from the external world back to the internal.

> **A person's true stability often begins when they stop seeking approval from everyone.**

## The Most Moving Thing About *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*: It is Both Realistic and Not Cruel

I like this book not because it is gentle. On the contrary, it can be shockingly cold at times.

It acknowledges that people are biased, make misjudgments, chase appearances, fawn over power, and are indifferent to the weak. It does not whitewash human nature, nor does it pretend the world is naturally fair. But it does not slide into despair because of this. It still believes that people can cultivate virtue through self-reflection; can maintain decency in a chaotic world; and can preserve a certain appropriateness and dignity within an imperfect reality.

This quality is very rare. It is neither chicken-soup optimism nor cynical cleverness. It is a mature clarity.

## Conclusion

After finishing *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, I didn't suddenly become a more noble person.

But I did become calmer.

I no longer obsess over every imbalance needing immediate correction, nor do I treat everyone's opinions as final verdicts. I have begun to accept that the world has its cold structures, that people have their limited sympathy, and that the only thing I am truly responsible for is whether the spectator within my own heart remains clear.

In this noisy and often distorted world, perhaps maturity is not about winning all the applause. *It is about finally having a judgment system that is not easily shattered by the outside world.*
