## ——From Laura, Peter, Danny, and Alana: Seeing How Relationships, Trauma, and Personality Are Formed

When first reading "Good Morning, Monster," many are shaken by the suffering experienced by the characters in the book. Abandonment, abuse, humiliation, war, cultural deprivation – these plot points are heavy enough on their own, as if each chapter reminds the reader: the human life can bear such immense weight.

But when I reopened this book, my attention no longer lingered on the events themselves. I began to care more about something else: how did these experiences later enter a person's personality, and how do they continue to operate in adult relationships?

So I gradually realized that "Good Morning, Monster" isn't really just about trauma. It's about how, after trauma, a person is shaped into who they are today.

## Laura: What Are Those Always Mending Relationships Really Mending?

In Laura's chapter, there's a line that struck me deeply. She described her family as an old Cuban car – long missing parts, yet still barely running through constant patching. Her life, she said, was like continuously searching for parts for that car, even if all she had was chewing gum, she had to find a way to keep it going.

What makes this line so moving is that it doesn't belong to Laura alone. In reality, many people live as such repair workers in their relationships.

They are always quicker than others to sense cracks. When the atmosphere changes, they immediately tense up; when a relationship cools, they lean in; when a partner's emotions are chaotic, they immediately take on the responsibility of soothing. Others often praise them for being mature, sensible, and good at managing relationships. But if we look deeper, this ability is often not just a strength, but a result of some past experience.

When a person lives in an unstable environment for a long time during early childhood, they gradually develop a belief: relationships don't get better on their own; someone must take responsibility for maintaining them. And that someone is often themselves.

Thus, love in their hearts is no longer just companionship and connection, but becomes responsibility, endurance, and repair. They are used to proving their worth by solving problems, and used to using excessive responsibility in exchange for the security of the relationship's continued existence.

This also explains why some people feel uncomfortable in healthy relationships. A stable, clear, emotionally mature partner should theoretically be reassuring, but for those who have long played the role of fixer, such a relationship can feel foreign. Because once there are no problems to solve, they don't know how else to be needed.

Many people mistakenly believe they cannot live without a certain person. In reality, what they cannot live without is the version of themselves that is always useful within that relationship.

## Peter and Danny: The Most Terrifying Thing About Trauma Is That It Continues to Flow

The stories of Peter and Danny showed me another, heavier reality: harm often does not stop with the generation that experienced it, but continues to flow downward.

The book mentions that many abusers were once victims themselves. This statement is not meant to excuse any harm, but to reveal a cruel truth: the violence, humiliation, and neglect in many families don't appear out of nowhere; they are old wounds reenacting in new forms over time.

A person who grew up being humiliated may express anger through humiliation; a person who was chronically ignored often doesn't know how to respond to others' emotional needs; a person who grew up in fear may easily mistake control for strength. The problem isn't just that they did something wrong, but that they never saw any other way.

Many people don't realize until adulthood that what they once thought was normal interaction was actually abnormal. That realization often comes with complex emotions: both anger and sadness; a desire to blame, and yet finally, understanding.

Danny's story pushes this harm to a cultural level. He was sent to a boarding school, forced to cut his long hair, forbidden to speak his native language, and shamed for his original identity. Reading these passages, I felt not just personal tragedy, but a sense of being uprooted, of drifting.

Language is never just a tool for communication. It carries memory, a sense of belonging, and the way one understands the world. When a person's language is taken away, what is lost is not just the ability to express, but the foundation of "who I am." So Danny's later表现出来的 aloofness, vigilance, and difficulty trusting are not just personality traits, but survival responses after long-term trauma.

## Alana: Why Do People, Even When Grown, Remain Controlled by the Past?

In Alana's chapter, there is a very precise metaphor. She said that abused children are like hunting dogs, constantly scanning the environment, looking for what might go wrong.

When I first read this, I thought of many adults in real life. They just seem sensitive, easily anxious, always overthinking. But if you look deeper, that is often not a character flaw, but a survival instinct trained long ago.

In a dangerous environment, the earlier you detect emotional changes, the more likely you are to reduce harm. So children learn to observe tone, predict conflict, gauge expressions, and develop vigilance into a skill. This might have protected them in childhood, but when they grow up, this system doesn't automatically shut down.

Thus, a delayed reply in a relationship might be interpreted as coldness; a change in tone might be felt as rejection; a silence might immediately trigger a fear of abandonment. Intellectually, they know nothing is probably wrong, but their bodies are already in a state of alert.

This is what many people find most painful. Reality is already safe, but the nervous system is still living in the past.

We often say someone is "too sensitive," but rarely ask: perhaps that sensitivity once saved them.

## What "Good Morning, Monster" Truly Reveals: How People Live Within Old Systems

After rereading the entire book, my biggest takeaway is that many people think they are making choices, but are actually just repeatedly running a certain system.

Laura was running a system of repair. She always felt relationships depended on her to maintain. Peter's family ran a system of violence and imbalance. Danny carried a system of disconnection after deprivation. Alana operated a surveillance system that never powered down.

This also explains why many people know a relationship is problematic but still can't leave; why they understand many truths but still repeatedly fall back into old patterns. Because understanding happens in the mind, but repetition often happens within the body and habits.

So growth is never just "figuring things out." Real change begins when a person starts to identify which reactions come from the past, which voices don't belong to their present self, and which relationships are actually built on long-term imbalance.

At that moment, they wake up for the first time from an automatically running life.

## Conclusion

What makes "Good Morning, Monster" so moving is not just how much darkness it portrays, but because it allows us to see: even if the past has deeply shaped a person, that doesn't necessarily have to be the final outcome.

Many adults carry anxieties, relational difficulties, and self-doubt they can't even articulate, often not because they don't try hard enough, but because old systems are still operating within them.

The past shaped you, but it doesn't have to keep driving you.
